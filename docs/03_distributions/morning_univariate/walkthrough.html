1.1 Working with the binomial distribution

Calculation function
get B from A \texttt{dbinom(A, N, p)}
\(P(X \ge A)\) \texttt{sum(dbinom(A:N, N, p))}
\(P(X \le A)\) \texttt{sum(dbinom(0:A, N, p))}
get X given C \texttt{qbinom(C, N, p, lower.tail = FALSE)}

where \(B=P(X=A)\), \(C\) is \(P(X \ge A)\) (i.e., the blue area), \(N\) is the number of trials, and \(p\) is the probability of “success”

1.2 BINOMIAL PROBLEMS

Your friend claims that her dog can correctly interpret her verbal requests to fetch five different kinds of objects: a squeaky toy, a newspaper, slippers, the TV remote, and a leash. You are a skeptic and decide to test her claim. You lay these five objects out on the floor in the living room of her flat. You run 100 “trials”, with each trial consisting of a request by your friend for the dog to bring an object from the living room to her bedroom (where she is located). On each trial you record the dog’s accuracy. Assume that the dog always returns with an object. There are 20 trials for each of the five objects, presented in a random order.

Assuming your friend’s dog has no language comprehension abilities:

  1. What is the probability of “success” (i.e., that the dog will be accurate on any single trial)?
  2. What is the probability that it will be accurate on exactly 30 trials?
  3. What is the probability that its accuracy will be between 15% and 25% (range includes 15 and 25)?
  4. What is the accuracy level beyond which you would reject the null hypothesis of no language abilities, assuming the test is one-tailed, \(\alpha=.05\)?

1.3 Probability of “success”   answer

Assuming your friend’s dog has no language comprehension abilities:

(1) What is the probability of “success” (i.e., that the dog will be accurate on any single trial)?

choices are (toy, newpaper, slippers, remote, leash)

\(p = \frac{1}{\text{number of choices}} = \frac{1}{5} = .2\)

1.8 NORMAL DISTRIBUTION PROBLEMS

The average height (μ) of a 16-24 year old Scotsman is 176.2 centimetres, with a standard deviation (σ) of 6.748. The average height (μ) of a 16-24 year old Scotswoman is 163.8 cm, with a standard deviation (σ) of 6.931. Assume that height is normally distributed. (Data from the Scottish Health Survey, 2008.)

For these problems, please use the \(z\)-score formula

\(z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\).

  1. What is the probability of meeting a 16-24 y.o. Scotswoman who is taller than the average 16-24 y.o. Scotsman?
  2. Fiona is a very tall Scotswoman (181.12 cm) in the 16-24 y.o. range who refuses to date men who are shorter than her. What proportion of all 16-24 Scotsmen would she be willing to date?
  3. Out of 100,000 16-24 y.o. Scotswomen, how many would you predict would meet the height eligibility requirement to join the Royal Navy (at least 151.5 cm tall)?
  4. How tall would a 16-24 y.o. Scotsman have to be in order to be in the top 5% of the height distribution for Scotsmen in his age group?

1.9 Scotswomen taller than typical Scotsman   answer R

(1) What is the probability of meeting a 16-24 y.o. Scotswoman who is taller than the average 16-24 y.o. Scotsman?

  mean SD
Scotswoman 163.8 6.931
Scotsman 176.2 6.748

\(z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\)

A 16-24yo Scotswoman who is as tall as the average Scotsman has a \(z\)-score of \(z = \frac{176.2-163.8}{6.931} = 1.789\), i.e., she is 1.789 \(SD\)s above the mean of her distribution. The probability of encountering a woman as tall as this or taller, \(P(X \ge 1.789)\), is:

  1. R code   B_block
    pnorm(1.789,lower.tail=FALSE)

    [1] 0.03680741

1.10 Fiona’s dating problems   R answer

(2) Fiona is a very tall Scotswoman (181.12 cm) in the 16-24 y.o. range who refuses to date men who are shorter than her. What proportion of all 16-24 Scotsmen would she be willing to date?

  mean SD
Scotswoman 163.8 6.931
Scotsman 176.2 6.748

\(z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\)

What proportion of Scotsmen are taller than Fiona? A z-score for a man as tall as she would be \(z = \frac{181.12-176.2}{6.748} = .729\). The proportion of men at least this tall is:

1.11 Royal Navy problem   R answer

(3) Out of 100,000 16-24 y.o. Scotswomen, how many would you predict would meet the height eligibility requirement to join the Royal Navy (at least 151.5 cm tall)?

mean SD
163.8 6.931

\(z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\)

A female who is 151.5 cm tall has a \(z\)-score of \(z = \frac{151.5-163.8}{6.931} = -1.774636\). The proportion of women taller than this is:

1.12 How tall to be in the top 5%?   answer R

(4) How tall would a 16-24 y.o. Scotsman have to be in order to be in the top 5% of the height distribution for Scotsmen in his age group?

  mean SD
Scotswoman 163.8 6.931
Scotsman 176.2 6.748

\(z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\)

What \(z\) score cuts off the top 5% of the distribution?

  1. R code   B_block
    z_crit <- qnorm(.05, lower.tail = FALSE)
print(z_crit)

    
[1] 1.644854

1.13 How tall to be in the top 5%? (cont’d)   answer

So, someone in the top 5% (95th percentile) has a \(z\)-score of at least 1.645. Now let’s solve the \(z\)-score formula for \(X\) to find out what the height would be.

Thus, a 16-24 y.o. Scotsman must be 187.3 cm tall or taller to be in the top 5% of the distribution.

  1. R code   B_block answer R
    z_crit * 6.748 + 176.2

    187.299472274669
